# Memory Consolidation

Memory consolidation is the process by which newly acquired, labile memories are transformed into stable, long-term representations. This occurs primarily during sleep.

## Two-Stage Model

1. **Fast encoding** — The hippocampus rapidly captures episodic memories during waking hours
2. **Slow consolidation** — During sleep, hippocampal memories are replayed and gradually integrated into neocortical networks

This maps to the [[complementary-learning-systems]] theory: the hippocampus acts as a fast learner, the neocortex as a slow learner.

## Sleep Stages

- **Slow-wave sleep (SWS)**: Hippocampal sharp-wave ripples drive memory reactivation
- **REM sleep**: Emotional memories are preferentially consolidated
- **Spindle-ripple coupling**: Thalamic spindles coordinate the transfer from hippocampus to cortex

## Implications for Knowledge Management

A knowledge vault mirrors this process:
- Quick captures (inbox) act as hippocampal encoding
- Periodic review and linking ([[spaced-repetition]]) acts as consolidation
- Mature, well-linked notes represent cortical knowledge
