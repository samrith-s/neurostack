# Active Recall

Active recall is the practice of stimulating memory retrieval during learning, rather than passively re-reading material. It is one of the most effective evidence-based learning strategies.

## Why It Works

Each retrieval attempt strengthens the memory trace through a distinct mechanism from initial encoding:
- **Retrieval practice effect**: Testing enhances long-term retention more than additional study (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006)
- **Desirable difficulty**: Effortful retrieval produces stronger memories than easy review
- **Elaborative retrieval**: The act of searching memory creates additional retrieval cues

## Connection to Prediction Errors

Active recall works because failed retrieval generates a [[prediction-errors-in-learning|prediction error]] — the brain expected to retrieve the information but couldn't. This error signal:
1. Flags the memory for priority consolidation during [[memory-consolidation|sleep]]
2. Creates additional encoding pathways
3. Strengthens the cue-target association

## Practical Techniques

- **Flashcards** with [[spaced-repetition]] scheduling
- **Free recall** after reading a chapter
- **Self-testing** before reviewing notes
- **Teaching others** (forces retrieval and reorganisation)

## Anti-Patterns

- Highlighting and re-reading (feels productive, isn't)
- Copying notes verbatim
- Studying without testing
